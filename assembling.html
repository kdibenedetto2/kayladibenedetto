Printing & Assembling our Prosthetic Hands

Printing


The first step in our protheses creation was printing. We were told to scale up the parts by 150% and fit them onto 3 plates. After importing the STL files, we had to orient them onto the plates. For some parts, this also involved rotating their position (which part touched the plate and which part didn't) so that they would print optimally. One part that was tricky about doing this was that since we were printing with supports, slicing the print would tell me that the design exceeded the plate's dimensions. Assembling


After printing all of our parts, we then organized them according to how it was done in the video so we had an idea of where everything was located in order to streamline the process (shown below). Once we did that, putting the pieces together was relatively straightforward, and we followed the PDF instructions to do so. Jake and I started with attaching the fingers and using their respective pins to do so. We used the following order: long, short, short, long.


After attaching the fingers, it was time to form the wrist cuff. Since the cuff prints as a flat piece due to the nature of 3D printing, we had to apply heat in order to shape it into the desired form. To do so, we brought our print over to Gianna, who used a heat gun on it (shown below). She applied heat while the print was in a mold that matched the correct shape of a wrist cuff. Once it was shaped correctly, we attached the wrist cuff to the hand using the wrist pins and wrist pin caps


After forming the wrist cuff, we got to move onto the final part of assembly: stringing the fingers through the hand. In accordance with the PDF instructions, we started with stringing the middle two fingers, using separate pieces of thin string. The string was threaded through the palm and down to the ends of the fingers, where it was then tied around small printed bars. After securing the strings for the middle fingers, we threaded the loose ends through the whippletree (a string-holding mechanism) and into the gripper box (a wrist-mounted holder for the whippletree). We repeated this process for the remaining fingers and the thumb, ensuring that all strings were properly tensioned (could move and grip properly but not too loose).

The final step was the installation of the demo bar. This component spans the palm and allows individuals with full wrist mobility to operate and test the prosthetic hand by flexing their wrist. With the demo bar in place, we successfully tested the hand's function. Overall, I think Jake and I were very happy with how our hand turned out!
